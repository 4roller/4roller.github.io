The TR580 CVT Transmission

CVT

The TR580 is a Subaru "Lineartronic" CVT (Continuously Variable Transmission). It is also sometimes confused with its older brother, the TR690, Subaru's high torque CVT which can be found in larger and more powerful Legacy's + Outback's NA 3.6 H6 and Turbo 2L WRX's, and 2.0L Levorg Wagons. The number 580 relates to the length of this case in millimeters. The two transmissions use a similar design. The transmission uses a metal chain and pulley system to vary the drive ratios. The TR690 is notoriously known for chain slip in high torque and modified applications. The TR580 on the other hand seems perfectly mated to the less-torquey Naturally Aspirated FB20/FB25 engines found in Subarus' Impreza, Crosstrek, Forester, and Outback. This transmission is categorized as an automatic and so, coupled with a torque converter. In most cases (non-base models) there is the option to utilize a 6 speed manual shift mode via/ steering wheel paddle shifters.

The CVT utilizes "Active Torque Split" which is an electronically controlled multi-plate transfer clutch that dynamically distributes power between front and rear axles. Anywhere from 60% F / 40% R for crusing or 50% F / 50% R on-demand. This allows for tranmission efficiency through better gas mileage. For comparison, the manual transmission center differential is always a mechanical 50% 50% F/R split.

Maintenance

For the most part the tranmission is reliable. It is often stated that the transmission fluid should be checked/replaced every 30k miles. This can help keep the fluid coupling of the pulley system and valving in good working order. Subaru recommends its own CVT fluid for the TR580 and should not be confused with High-Torque Fluid (orange-red in colour). In my expereince, I had the dealer flush and fill the CVT fluid at 30k. The difference was a smoother, quieter and tighter power deliver. I suspect the original fluid had debris from transmission break-in.

Reliability

Heat is the number one enemy of this transmision. The OEM CVT heater/cooler "can" (right) is tied to the coolant system via an water/oil heat exchange. In some cases, during prolonged periods of high load, the heat exchanger system is insufficient and can be easily overloaded. The CVT fluid becomes too hot and trips an electronic "limp mode" which limits engine RPMs to below 4000 and only allows the CVT to run in less taxing gear ratios. For this reason it is recommended that a CVT cooler (below) be installed anticipating high engine load driving be it mountain passes or spirited canyon driving. An inline method (utilizes oem cooler) or the bypass method can be used depending on climate zone you live in. I use a Derale 19 row staked plate cooler, but really any transmission cooler would work. Changing fluid at 30-60k intervals and keeping the tranmission cool will help prolong the life of the CVT. A common issue is CVT solinoid failure and has been reported anywhere between 80-300k miles so your mileage may vary. Otherwise the transmission should prove stout enough for the every day commoner.

The CVT has an automatic safety check which does not let the engine over rev. This is paticually useful for novice/advanced drivers as there is no possibility going past red line. An example of this safety feature is an auditory beep denoting NO-SHIFT when the driver has requested a ratio shift (downshift with paddles), but the engine is already above 4000rpm.
