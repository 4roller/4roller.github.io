The TR580 Transmission

The TR580 is a Subaru "Lineartronic" CVT. The number 580 relates to its length in millimeters. The transmission uses a metal chain and pulley system to vary the drive ratios. This transmission is categorized as an automatic and so, coupled with a torque converter. There is the option to utilize a 6 speed manual shift mode via/ steering wheel paddle shifters.

The CVT utilizes "Active Torque Split" which is an electronically controlled multi-plate transfer clutch that dynamically distributes power between front and rear axles. Anywhere from 60% F / 40% R for crusing or 50% F / 50% R on-demand. This allows the tranmission efficiency and better gas mileage. For comparison, the manual transmission center differential is always a mechanical 50% 50% F/R split.

Maintenance

For the most part the tranmission is reliable. It is often stated that the transmission fluid should be checked/replaced every 30k miles. This can help keep the fluid coupling of the pulley system and valving in good working order. In my expereince, I had the dealer replace the CVT fluid at 30k. The difference was a smoother, quieter and tighter power deliver. I suspect the original fluid had debris from transmission break-in.

Reliability

Heat is the number one enemy of this transmision. The OEM CVT heater/cooler "can" (right) is tied to the coolant system via an water/oil heat exchange. In some cases, during prolonged periods of high load, the heat exchanger system is insufficient. The CVT fluid becomes too hot and trips an electronic "limp mode" which limits engine RPMs to below 4000 and only allows the CVT to run in less taxing gear ratios. For this reason it is recommended that a CVT cooler (below) be installed. An inline method (utilizes oem cooler) or the bypass method can be used depending on climate zone you live in. I use a Derale 19 row staked plate cooler, but really any transmission cooler would work.

Performance

Another way to extract the most fun out of the transmission is to utilize the 6 speed manual mode via paddle shifters. By using manual mode, it is possible to shift the transmission like a sequential gearbox. Manual mode also allows the driver to rev up to ~6000rpms before the transmission automatically shifts into the next gear ratio. While using manual mode it is also possible to hold RPMs for engine braking or for cornering holeshot. The CVT has an automatic safety check which does not let the engine over rev. This is paticually useful for novice/advanced drivers as there is no possibility of grenading the engine from going past red line. The singal for NO-SHIFT is denoted as a beep that comes from the dashboard when the driver has requestd a ratio shift (downshift), but the engine is already above 4000rpm.
